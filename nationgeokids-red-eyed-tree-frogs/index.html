Red-eyed Tree Frogs: Rainforest Flash of Colour

Text 2 — Nat Geo Kids Adapted B2 Reading

🐸 🌿 👁️

[1] Red-eyed tree frogs live in tropical rainforests across Central America, from southern Mexico down to Panama. They are small tree-dwelling amphibians famous for shocking bright colour patterns: vivid lime-green backs, orange webbed feet, blue striped sides and huge glowing scarlet eyes. Many local rainforest predators hunt small frogs, but this frog's bright colours are not used to attract mates — they act as a fast self-defence trick called startle colouration to scare attackers away.

[2] These frogs spend nearly their whole lives high up on rainforest foliage, rarely touching the forest floor. Their suction-cup toe pads stick firmly to smooth wet leaves and thin tree branches, letting them climb vertically without slipping. They only descend to shallow rainforest streams once a year during breeding season to lay eggs. Their thin moist skin soaks up oxygen from humid air, which means they cannot survive in dry, hot open spaces far from tree cover.

[3] The special startle defence works in a simple effective way. During daylight hours, red-eyed tree frogs rest motionless on green leaves, tucking their bright orange feet and blue body stripes underneath their green backs. Only their green skin shows, perfectly camouflaged against surrounding foliage. If a bird, snake or lizard disturbs them, they suddenly jump and pop open their massive red eyes, flashing all four bright colour sets at once. Most predators get briefly shocked and pause, giving the frog enough time to leap away and hide deep inside thick leaves.

[4] Breeding cycles rely on seasonal rain floods. Male frogs gather beside small rainforest streams and make soft vibrating croaks to draw female partners. Females lay jelly-covered egg clusters on the bottom sides of overhanging leaves above stream water. When tadpoles hatch two weeks later, the jelly dissolves and tiny young slide straight down into the stream to feed on water algae. Tadpoles slowly grow legs and lose their tails over one month before climbing back up trees as fully formed small frogs.

[5] Deforestation creates the biggest long-term threat to red-eyed tree frog populations. Farmers cut down old rainforest trees to create cattle fields and crop plantations, removing the leaf canopy shelter and shallow breeding streams the frogs need to live. Pesticides sprayed on nearby farmland wash into forest streams, poisoning tadpoles and damaging the frogs' thin oxygen-absorbing skin. Climate change also makes rainforest dry seasons longer, drying up small breeding water pools before tadpoles mature.

[6] Local rainforest protection projects help save these colourful frogs. National rainforest reserves ban large-scale tree cutting and limit pesticide use near stream systems. Youth volunteer groups plant native rainforest saplings to rebuild lost tree canopy habitats. Tour companies run quiet guided rainforest walks with strict rules to avoid touching frog leaves or disturbing breeding egg clusters.

[7] Red-eyed tree frogs are important indicator species for rainforest health. Their thin sensitive skin quickly reacts to water and air pollution, so falling frog numbers warn scientists that the whole rainforest ecosystem is growing unbalanced. Protecting these tiny colourful amphibians protects thousands of other rainforest insects, birds and reptiles that share the same tree-top habitat.
