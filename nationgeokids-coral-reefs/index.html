[1] Many people mistake coral for colourful underwater plants, yet corals are actually tiny soft-bodied sea animals called polyps. Each polyp builds a hard limestone skeleton at its base. When thousands of polyps grow and connect together over thousands of years, they form massive rocky structures known as coral reefs. These reefs only develop in shallow, warm, clear seawater near the Equator because they rely on sunlight to survive.

[2] Coral reefs are widely nicknamed “the rainforests of the sea” due to their incredible biodiversity. Even though reefs take up less than one percent of the total ocean floor, they provide shelter and food for around 25 percent of all marine species. Countless creatures such as sea turtles, seahorses, giant clams and reef fish make their homes among coral branches. Many small sea animals use coral’s twisting shapes to hide from larger predators.

[3] A special symbiotic partnership keeps coral alive and bright. Microscopic algae named zooxanthellae live inside coral polyps. The algae use sunlight to make food through photosynthesis and share nutrients with the coral. In return, the coral gives the algae a safe home. It is these tiny algae that create the vivid red, blue and orange colours we see on healthy reefs. Without zooxanthellae, corals turn pale white, a harmful process called coral bleaching.

[4] The Great Barrier Reef in Australia is the world’s biggest coral reef system. It stretches over 2,300 kilometres and is so large it can be seen from outer space. Scientists have found that the oldest reef layers date back more than 240 million years, existing long before dinosaurs walked on land. Over 800 types of hard coral grow within this huge natural landmark.

[5] Sadly, coral reefs face multiple serious threats today. First, global climate change raises ocean temperatures. Hot water forces corals to expel their zooxanthellae, causing mass bleaching events. Second, farm chemical pollution and plastic waste flow into coastal waters, poisoning polyps. Third, careless boat anchors and destructive fishing methods smash fragile coral structures into broken rubble.

[6] Losing coral reefs brings heavy damage to both nature and humans. Around 500 million people worldwide depend on reef fish as their main food source. Reefs also act as natural sea walls that absorb powerful storm waves, stopping floods and erosion along coastal towns. If reefs disappear, coastal communities will face more dangerous typhoons and shortages of seafood.

[7] People around the globe are carrying out practical restoration work to save dying reefs. Marine scientists grow healthy coral fragments in underwater nurseries then plant them onto damaged rock beds. Governments create protected marine parks where destructive fishing and boating are banned. Ordinary citizens can also help by cutting single-use plastic and choosing seafood caught in eco-friendly ways.
