Text 1 — SCMP Young Post B2 Level Digital Well-being

📱 🧘 📉

[1] Every day, Hong Kong teenagers spend an average of six to seven hours glued to screens, scrolling through social media, playing online games or watching short video clips. Health officials have released worrying statistics showing that more than 60 per cent of secondary students use digital devices for entertainment for at least two hours on school days. Many educators and parents are alarmed by this trend, which shrinks attention spans and weakens face-to-face communication skills.

[2] The common solution most families choose is strict screen time limits. Parents lock phones away before dinner or ban devices in bedrooms. However, many young people argue that rigid rules only trigger conflicts and secret phone use. Alyssa, a 19-year-old university student, created an online community called Digital Minimalism to offer a different approach. Instead of fully cutting off technology, its members learn to use digital tools intentionally, only keeping online services that match their core values.

[3] The group now has nearly 40,000 local and overseas members. Participants share small victories: finishing a school project without distracting pop-up notifications, navigating city streets without relying on map apps, or having long conversations with friends without checking phones every minute. Aly explains that digital minimalism does not mean rejecting all technology. It encourages people to eliminate meaningless passive scrolling that brings no real joy or useful information.

[4] Psychologists support this philosophy. A recent youth study found constant passive browsing raises anxiety and lowers the ability to focus on long tasks. Our brains become used to fast-changing short content, making thick textbooks or lengthy articles feel boring and unappealing. Many students admit they cannot finish a reading task without repeatedly picking up their phones for quick breaks.

[5] Experts also point out that over-dependence on digital tools erodes practical life skills. Young people who always use translation apps rarely memorise new vocabulary. Those who save every photo on cloud storage lose the habit of printing pictures to share with family. When technology takes over basic daily tasks, teenagers lose independence and creative thinking.

[6] Schools have started to introduce digital well-being lessons as an alternative to harsh device bans. One popular student-designed activity is a card game named Tech Talk. It creates a safe space for teens to talk honestly about online addiction, harmful online content and feelings of loneliness caused by social media comparison. Teachers report that open discussion works far better than strict punishment, as students voluntarily recognise their unhealthy online habits.

[7] Still, digital minimalism does not demand complete disconnection from the online world. Technology can be valuable for study, staying in touch with distant relatives and discovering new hobbies. The key difference lies in conscious, active use rather than mindless scrolling. People should ask themselves whether each app or social page truly improves their daily life before keeping it on their devices.
