Could we ditch fast fashion?

Text 1 — BBC Learning English 6 Minute English / DSE-style Practice

👕 🌍 👖

[1] Fast fashion describes cheap, mass-produced clothes that copy the latest runway trends and flood high-street shops every week. This business model boomed in the 1960s, after factories gained fast sewing technology and brands moved production overseas to find cheap labour. Companies chase the cheapest needle, meaning they constantly hunt manufacturers who can cut costs lower than competitors, driving clothing prices down for shoppers.

[2] Low prices create a throwaway culture. Clothing production doubled between 2000 and 2015, yet the average garment is worn 36 per cent fewer times than decades ago. Many people feel invisible social pressure from social media to buy new outfits for photos; they avoid repeating the same clothes online, even if the old items are still in perfect condition.

[3] Fast fashion carries severe environmental costs. A single cotton T-shirt requires 2,700 litres of water to produce. Factories dump toxic dye chemicals into local rivers, poisoning wildlife and drinking water supplies. Synthetic fabrics do not break down easily, so millions of unwanted clothes end up in landfills where they take up to 200 years to decompose. The fashion industry’s total carbon output exceeds international flights and sea shipping combined.

[4] The human cost is equally shocking. The 2013 Rana Plaza factory collapse in Bangladesh killed over 1,100 garment workers, exposing unsafe buildings, extremely long shifts and tiny wages for staff. Many brands ignore worker rights to keep their production expenses minimal, despite public campaigns demanding fair pay and safe workplaces.

[5] In recent years, sustainable alternatives have grown popular to fight fast fashion’s harm. Buying second-hand clothing is the simplest choice. Reports predict the resale market will double the size of fast fashion by 2030. Online resale platforms and charity shops give pre-loved garments a new life, and celebrities now promote thrifting on mainstream TV shows, changing the old idea that second-hand clothes are only for people with little money.

[6] Experts from environmental charity WRAP argue people should repair and revamp their existing wardrobes before purchasing anything new. Clothing libraries let customers borrow premium outfits instead of buying them. Some brands also launch circular fashion lines: these items are built from biodegradable materials and designed to be recycled once they wear out.

[7] However, shoppers must watch out for “greenwashing”. Many big fashion labels release one small eco-friendly capsule collection each year while producing thousands of new fast fashion pieces monthly. They use vague words like “eco” or “sustainable” without clear proof of real change, tricking buyers into believing they support ethical production.

12. Why is greenwashing misleading for consumers? (line 7)

12 Short Answer 2 marks

Because big brands only release one small eco collection each year while still producing thousands of new fast fashion items monthly. They use vague words like "eco" without any clear proof of real environmental change, so they trick buyers into thinking they are ethical.

Greenwashing means pretending to be eco-friendly without actually making meaningful changes to deceive consumers.

大廠商每年只出一個小型環保系列，但每月仍然生產成千上萬件新快時尚衣服。他們只用 "eco" 這種模糊字眼，沒有真正改變的明確證據，誤導消費者以為他們支持道德生產。

[7] Many big fashion labels release one small eco-friendly capsule collection each year while producing thousands of new fast fashion pieces monthly. They use vague words like "eco" or "sustainable" without clear proof of real change, tricking buyers into believing they support ethical production.
13. What does the writer imply about large fashion brands that release small eco collections?

13 Inference 2 marks

The writer implies that these small eco collections are just for marketing and do not fix the core environmental and ethical harm caused by their large-scale fast fashion production.

The writer puts "greenwashing" in quotes and explains that brands trick buyers, so he implies the small collections don't solve the real problem.

作者指出這就是「漂綠」，品牌只是欺騙消費者，小環保系列只是營銷手法，並未解決大規模快時尚生產帶來的核心環境和道德傷害。

[7] However, shoppers must watch out for "greenwashing". Many big fashion labels release one small eco-friendly capsule collection each year while producing thousands of new fast fashion pieces monthly. They use vague words... tricking buyers into believing they support ethical production.
