Listen Up: Japanese security guard turns duct tape into famous signs

Text 1 — DSE Reading Practice

📖 ✏️

[1] One of Japan's most in-demand graphic designers is not quite hip or an artist by training. He is a septuagenarian security guard who works the graveyard shift at a railway station in the country's capital, Tokyo. Shuetsu Sato found his hidden passion in 2004. He was asked to help travellers navigate the crowded, labyrinthine Shinjuku Station in the city's major commercial centre as it underwent renovations.

[2] Sato's solution was to make signage out of duct tape. He covered the station's walls with directions to the myriad train lines, exits and more. It was effective. The station master loved the work and asked for more. As more signs went up, discerning eyes noticed. Soon, the 72-year-old was getting requests from all over Japan to make lettering for film posters and even a museum sign. He even got work from corporate giants such as Nintendo and Suntory.

[3] Last year, he won the Japan Sign Design Association's platinum award for his unique typography, now known as "Shuetsu-tai" after his name. It is characterised by rounded edges designed to soothe commuters' irritation.

[4] One of Sato's latest projects is collaborating on a logo for Nike's new bricks-and-mortar store, which opened in April in the Shinjuku shopping district. Shun Sasaki, a graphic designer hired by the sporting goods giant, said he immediately thought of Sato when he was asked to come up with a logo that evoked Shinjuku. Sasaki said he was blown away by Sato's cool, powerful lettering and that the security guard-turned-artist was overflowing with incredible passion and a strong desire to create.

[5] For all his work's popularity, Sato has never charged a set fee, leaving compensation up to his clients. He sometimes gets so lost in making signs that he forgets to eat or sleep. So he only asks for meals to be provided while he works, for transport costs to be covered and for the understanding that he will only use duct tape to create his designs.

[6] Sato said he received a certificate of appreciation from the station master and a shampoo set for his work on Shinjuku Station. Another station master that he did signage for thanked him with new socks. Sato said that he could not help but laugh at that. He added that creating these signs is fun for him and as long as it made people happy, that was really all he needed.
