Should we eat ultra-processed food?

Text 1 — BBC Learning English 6 Minute English / DSE-style Practice

🍔 🥗 🥡

[1] What is your ultra-processed guilty pleasure? Many of us love home-cooked meals made from scratch, but when we are in a rush after work or school, it is far more convenient to grab ready meals, packaged snacks or frozen dishes from supermarkets. These quick options are classed as ultra-processed food, and modern medical research has linked frequent consumption of these products to long-term poor health.

[2] Ultra-processed food goes through heavy industrial production. It usually carries extremely long ingredient labels full of artificial sweeteners, preservatives, colour enhancers and stabilisers. Typical examples include chicken nuggets, mass-produced pizza, ice cream, fizzy soft drinks and flavoured breakfast cereals. Dr Mathilde Touvier, a French public health researcher, led two large-scale studies tracking over 100,000 adults for decades. Her team proved that participants who ate the most ultra-processed food faced higher risks of heart disease and premature death.

[3] Despite these worrying results, ultra-processed food still dominates supermarket shelves, mostly produced by multinational corporations known as “Big Food”. These top 25 food and beverage companies own famous global snack and fast-food brands, and they hold massive marketing clout to promote their low-cost, addictive goods to all age groups, especially teenagers. However, consumer attitudes are slowly shifting. More shoppers now choose fresh, organic and locally grown alternatives, which means Big Food brands are steadily losing market share year after year.

[4] Critics say Big Food firms feel disdain towards public health advice, as their profit model depends on cheap, highly processed ingredients. Yet the story of processed food is not completely negative. Food processing was first invented thousands of years ago to solve practical problems. Ancient civilisations salted, dried and fermented food to prevent food poisoning and make crops last far longer through cold winters. Minimal processing such as pasteurising milk or boiling beans removes natural toxins that would otherwise make humans sick.

[5] The main trouble lies in ultra-processing, not light food treatment. When food is rebuilt from isolated industrial chemicals instead of whole natural ingredients, it loses most fibre, vitamins and minerals. Many people admit ultra-processed snacks taste moreish and yummy, but they feel deep regret and guilt after eating them, knowing they are empty calories with little nutritional value.

[6] Restaurants have also reacted to changing public tastes. Fine dining used to be the only space for healthy, fresh cooking, but new casual eateries like Food Garage now serve balanced, unprocessed meals to young people who want tasty food without health risks. These small local restaurants offer a middle ground between classic fast food and expensive high-end dining.

[7] Nutrition experts do not demand people cut ultra-processed food out entirely. The principle of “everything in moderation” applies here: enjoying packaged treats once in a while will not damage your body. The real issue is constant daily reliance on ready-made meals, which crowds fruit, vegetables and whole grains out of people’s regular diets.

[8] To win back lost customers, Big Food companies are launching reform projects. They form buyer consortiums to source certified environmentally friendly raw materials, grow fresh herbs in on-site farms, and run staff training, customer reward schemes and product innovation programmes to reduce artificial additives in their lines. These changes aim to rebuild their damaged public reputation and stop further loss of market share.
