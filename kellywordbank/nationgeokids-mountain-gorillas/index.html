Reading Solution

Mountain Gorillas – Gentle Giants of African Cloud Forests

Text 2 — Nat Geo Kids Adapted B2 Reading

🦍 🌿 ⛰️

[1] Mountain gorillas live high up in misty cloud-covered rainforests in Rwanda, Uganda and the Democratic Republic of the Congo. These large primates have thick long black fur to survive cold mountain nights, when temperatures often drop close to freezing point. Two separate groups exist: lowland gorillas that live in warm flat jungles, and mountain gorillas adapted for high-altitude steep slopes. For decades, mountain gorillas were listed as critically endangered, with fewer than 1,000 wild individuals left on Earth.

[2] Every gorilla troop follows a clear social structure led by one mature male silverback gorilla. Silverbacks get their name from the grey silver fur that grows across their backs once they reach twelve years old. The troop leader protects all females, young gorillas and babies from leopards and rival gorilla groups. Each troop usually has 5 to 30 members, and all gorillas sleep together in leaf nests built on tree branches or thick forest ground every evening.

[3] Mountain gorillas are herbivores that eat over 140 kinds of wild forest plants. Their daily diet includes bamboo shoots, soft fruit, leafy stems and wild flowers. Their powerful jaws and large flat molars easily crush tough bamboo stalks that few other forest animals can digest. Unlike aggressive movie stereotypes, wild mountain gorillas are calm and peaceful; they only beat their chests and roar to scare away threats, never to attack without reason.

[4] Three major human pressures destroyed gorilla populations over the last 50 years. Farmers cut down cloud forest trees to expand crop fields and cattle land, wiping out the plant food gorillas rely on. Illegal poachers hunt gorillas to sell their body parts on black wildlife markets. Civil conflict in central Africa also forces local families to move into gorilla habitats, disturbing their quiet troop life and destroying nesting zones.

[5] Cross-border wildlife organisations launched long-term conservation projects to save mountain gorillas. Anti-poaching patrol teams carry daily forest walks to stop illegal hunters and remove hidden animal traps. Eco-tourism programmes let small guided visitor groups watch gorillas from a safe 10-metre distance, and all tour fees fund forest protection and local village schools. National park borders are marked with clear fences to stop farm expansion into wild gorilla territory.

[6] Local village communities are key to successful gorilla protection. Park authorities pay local families income to guard forest edges instead of clearing trees for farming. Youth training workshops teach villagers sustainable farming skills that do not destroy cloud forest habitats. Many former poachers now work as wildlife guides or patrol rangers, earning steady legal income from gorilla tourism.

[7] Mountain gorillas are irreplaceable keystone species for highland cloud forests. As they travel and feed across mountain slopes, they spread fruit seeds through their waste, helping hundreds of rare forest plant species grow across empty cleared land. Saving mountain gorillas means protecting the whole misty mountain forest ecosystem for countless birds, insects and small mammals that share their home.
