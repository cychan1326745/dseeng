Flamingos: Pink Birds of Salt Lagoons

Text 1 — Nat Geo Kids Adapted B2 Reading

🦩 🌅 💧

[1] Flamingos are famous for their bright pink-red feathers and long curved necks, living in shallow salt lakes and coastal lagoons across Africa, South America and Southern Europe. Many people wrongly believe flamingos are born pink; in fact, baby chicks hatch with plain grey down feathers. Their signature colourful plumage only develops after years of eating tiny water creatures filled with natural orange-red pigments called carotenoids. Without this special diet, adult flamingos turn pale white over time.

[2] Their whole body is perfectly adapted to feed in muddy salty water. Long flexible necks let them dip their heads fully underwater upside down. A unique curved beak filters mud, salt and waste out of water, trapping small shrimp, algae and insect larvae as food. Their long thin legs have tough scaly skin that resists sharp salt crystals and hot muddy ground. Webbed feet help them stand steady on soft mud and swim short distances across lagoons.

[3] Flamingos live in huge noisy flocks, sometimes gathering groups of over one hundred thousand birds. Living in massive crowds brings two key survival advantages. First, hundreds of pairs watch for predators such as eagles and wild foxes at the same time, so danger is spotted instantly. Second, large flocks share warmth during cold nights, protecting fragile chicks from freezing winds near open water. Every flock member communicates with loud honking calls to stay connected.

[4] Breeding season follows strict group rules. All flamingos in one lagoon lay eggs within the same short window, so all chicks grow up together. Pairs build small cone-shaped mounds from mud to lift eggs above floodwater and hot salt soil. Each couple only lays one single egg per year; both parents take turns sitting on the egg to keep it warm for around thirty days. After hatching, adults feed their babies a special nutrient-rich liquid produced inside their throats.

[5] Human activity creates growing threats to wild flamingo populations. Salt mining companies drain shallow lagoons to collect mineral salt, wiping out the tiny shrimp and algae flamingos rely on for food. Tourists walking too close to nesting mounds scare parent birds away, leaving unhatched eggs exposed to heat and predators. Plastic waste floating on lagoon surfaces also traps young flamingos and poisons their food sources.

[6] Global conservation groups carry out practical protection work. Local governments mark off protected no-entry nesting zones around salt lagoons, banning mining and uncontrolled tourist access. Scientists build artificial shallow feeding pools filled with shrimp to replace lost natural lagoon habitats. Tour operators run guided distant-watching tours so visitors can admire flamingos without disturbing their homes.

[7] Flamingos are more than just colourful sightseeing birds. Their presence signals clean, balanced wetland ecosystems. If flamingos disappear from a lagoon, it proves the water environment has become damaged by human pollution or habitat loss. Protecting these pink water birds means saving fragile wetland homes for thousands of other small water creatures too.
