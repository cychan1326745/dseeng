Wildlife of the Galápagos Islands

Text 1 — Nat Geo Kids Adapted B2 Reading

🐢 🌊 🦎

[1] The Galápagos Islands lie around 1,000 kilometres off the west coast of Ecuador in the Pacific Ocean. This remote archipelago is famous for its unique wild animals that cannot be found anywhere else on Earth. Because the islands are so far from the mainland, most species evolved separately, developing special traits to survive rocky shores, hot dry land and cold ocean currents. Scientists and wildlife lovers travel globally to visit this natural treasure, which is protected as a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

[2] Galápagos sea lions are one of the most common animals here. They are related to California sea lions and share dog-like barks to greet visitors. Adult sea lions can swim up to 15 kilometres out to hunt fish. Baby pups gather in shallow water nurseries to play and learn swimming skills. If annoying flies disturb their afternoon rest, tiny lava lizards run over to eat the insects, creating a helpful natural partnership.

[3] Blue-footed boobies are the most well-known birds native to these islands. Their strange name comes from the Spanish word "bobo", meaning fool or clown, due to their funny mating dances. When diving for food, they plunge from heights of 100 metres and hit the water at nearly 100 kilometres per hour. Males whistle to communicate while females make deep honking sounds. Over half of the global blue-footed booby population lives only in the Galápagos.

[4] Giant tortoises are the archipelago's iconic land creatures. Different island groups host separate tortoise species with differently shaped shells. These slow reptiles can live for more than 100 years and survive months without food or water by storing liquid inside their bodies. In the past, sailors captured thousands of tortoises to carry as fresh meat on long voyages, causing huge population losses. Strict international hunting bans have helped their numbers recover slowly.

[5] Human tourism creates both advantages and risks for the Galápagos ecosystem. On the positive side, eco-tourism brings money to local communities, which funds wildlife protection patrols and park rangers. However, uncontrolled visitor numbers bring hidden dangers: waste plastic pollutes coastlines, foreign animals like cats and rats eat native bird eggs, and too many footsteps damage fragile plant roots on volcanic soil.

[6] Strict park rules limit tourist activity to solve these threats. All visitors must join official guided tours and cannot walk alone off marked paths. Single-use plastic bags and bottles are completely forbidden on every island. Tour boats also carry waste storage tanks so no rubbish is thrown into the sea during trips.

[7] Ordinary young people can support Galápagos conservation without travelling there. Learn about unique island wildlife, avoid buying souvenirs made from wild animal shells or bones, and donate to official wildlife charities that fund anti-poaching work. Protecting the Galápagos means saving thousands of one-of-a-kind species that exist nowhere else on our planet.
