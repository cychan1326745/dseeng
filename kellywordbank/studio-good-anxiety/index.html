Good Anxiety: Not Just Your Enemy

Text 1 — Studio Classroom B2 Level

📚 🎵 🌍

[1] Most teenagers see anxiety as a terrible negative feeling. When exams or public speeches come close, they suffer from racing thoughts, sleepless nights and a heavy sense of pressure. Many students try every possible way to escape anxious moods: scrolling social media for hours, putting off revision or avoiding speaking in front of classmates. Neuroscientist Wendy Suzuki wrote the book Good Anxiety to change this common misunderstanding.

[2] Suzuki explains that anxiety is a natural human emotion, not a flaw in our minds. It acts as an internal warning system. Small levels of anxiety tell us we care deeply about a goal, such as passing an important test or getting along with friends. This gentle nervousness pushes us to prepare carefully instead of becoming lazy and careless. Without any anxiety at all, people would lack motivation to finish challenging tasks.

[3] However, there is a clear dividing line between healthy mild anxiety and harmful severe stress. Chronic long-term anxiety damages sleep quality, weakens memory and makes people lose interest in daily hobbies. When worry grows too strong, it stops students from concentrating during lessons, stops them from joining group activities and even ruins their appetite. These bad effects happen when people keep worrying without taking practical action to solve their troubles.

[4] Suzuki shares simple methods to turn anxious feelings into strength. First, name your worry clearly instead of pushing it away. Writing down your fears on paper stops them from circling endlessly in your brain. Second, split big stressful tasks into tiny easy steps. If you feel anxious about a whole exam subject, just finish one reading exercise each day instead of thinking about all the content at once.

[5] Third, set short daily rest time. Many teens keep studying for 8 hours straight without breaks, which lets anxiety build up rapidly. A 10-minute walk outside or light music can calm the nervous system fast. Physical exercise like jogging also releases happy chemicals in the brain to balance anxious feelings.

[6] Schools can also help students handle anxiety properly. More group sharing sessions should be held, so teenagers realise they are not alone in their worries. Teachers should avoid comparing every student’s exam scores publicly, which increases competitive pressure and triggers heavy anxiety.

[7] Anxiety is not something we need to completely remove from our lives. If we learn to recognise its two sides and manage it well, nervous feelings can become a powerful driving force for personal growth and better academic performance.
