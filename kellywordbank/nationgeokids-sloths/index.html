Text 1 — Nat Geo Kids B2 Level

🌿 📚 🌍

[1] Sloths (樹懶) are the slowest mammals living on Earth, native to Central and South American tropical rainforests. Two main groups exist: two-toed sloths and three-toed sloths. They spend more than 95 per cent of their entire lives hanging upside down from tall rainforest canopy branches, barely touching the ground at all. Their extremely slow movement saves precious energy, as their diet only supplies tiny amounts of nutrients.

[2] A sloth’s daily routine follows a fixed slow rhythm. They sleep for up to 20 hours every single day. When awake, they only creep short distances to grab soft tree leaves, young shoots and ripe fruit — their only food sources. Digesting one meal can take a whole month because leaf nutrition levels are very low. Sloths rarely drink liquid water; they absorb moisture straight from the green foliage they eat.

[3] Sloths have a unique built-in camouflage system. Tiny green algae grow naturally across their thick coarse fur, matching the colour of rainforest leaves. This natural green layer hides them from sharp-eyed predators such as harpy eagles and wild jungle cats. Sloth fur also has special grooves that trap rainwater and keep their bodies dry during tropical downpours.

[4] Moving on land puts sloths in serious danger. Their long curved claws are perfectly shaped for gripping tree branches but make walking clumsy and exhausting. On the ground, sloths can only drag themselves forward using their front limbs, which makes them easy targets for hunters and wild animals. They only climb down to the forest floor once a week to dig a small hole and release waste, then quickly scramble back up the trees.

[5] Female sloths give birth while hanging upside down in trees. The baby sloth clings tightly to its mother’s belly fur for its first six months of life, learning which leaves are safe to eat and how to move slowly without drawing predator attention. Young sloths separate from their mothers once they master basic survival skills in the canopy.

[6] Sloth populations face growing threats from human activity. Farmers cut rainforest trees to create farmland, destroying the canopy habitats sloths fully depend on. Illegal wildlife traders capture sloths to sell as exotic pets; many captured sloths die quickly without their natural leaf diet. Local wildlife groups set up protected forest reserves and run rescue centres for injured sloths.

[7] Ordinary tourists and students can protect wild sloths. Never buy sloth pets or souvenirs made from rainforest animals. Choose eco-tour operators that avoid disturbing wild canopy wildlife. Support rainforest reforestation charities to rebuild sloth homes that were cleared for farming. If human pressure eases, sloth numbers can recover over time.
