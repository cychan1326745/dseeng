Nat Geo Kids Adapted B2 Reading Passage 1 Plastic in the Ocean

Text 1 — Nat Geo Kids B2 Level

🌿 📚 🌍

[1] Plastic is one of the most useful human-made materials, cheap to produce and widely used in packaging, toys and daily goods. However, around half of all plastic items are designed for single use only, such as shopping bags, food wrappers and disposable water bottles. Once thrown away, most plastic waste fails to enter recycling systems and eventually flows into rivers, then empties into the world’s oceans. Every year, roughly 8 million tonnes of plastic trash enters marine environments. Plastic takes up to 400 years to fully break down, so it lingers in seawater for centuries and causes lasting damage to sea life.

[2] Over 700 marine species face severe threats from ocean plastic. Large animals like sea turtles often confuse floating plastic bags for jellyfish, their main food source. After swallowing plastic, turtles suffer blocked digestive systems and slowly starve to death. Tiny microplastics – small fragments formed when big plastic items decay – are eaten by fish, crabs and plankton at the bottom of the food chain. Toxic chemicals inside plastic accumulate in these small creatures and pass upward to bigger predators, including humans who eat seafood.

[3] Ocean plastic also changes seawater chemistry. The world’s seas naturally absorb carbon dioxide from the air to balance global temperatures. When massive piles of plastic waste rot underwater, extra CO2 is released into the ocean. This process makes seawater more acidic. Acidic water weakens the hard shells of crabs, lobsters and coral polyps, making it hard for them to grow and survive.

[4] Beach pollution is another visible result of plastic waste. Coastlines worldwide are covered with discarded bottles, straws and foam containers. Tourists avoid dirty beaches, which hurts local coastal tourism businesses. Cleaning crews spend thousands of working hours every year picking up plastic rubbish, costing cities huge sums of public money.

[5] Many governments have introduced new rules to cut single-use plastic. Some coastal cities ban disposable plastic bags and plastic drinking straws in restaurants. Shops must charge customers extra fees for plastic packaging to encourage reusable cloth bags. Marine protected zones also ban plastic waste dumping along shorelines.

[6] Ordinary teenagers can take easy daily actions to reduce plastic pollution. Carry reusable metal water bottles and cloth shopping bags when going out. Choose unpackaged fresh food instead of pre-wrapped snacks. Join beach clean-up volunteer groups on weekends to clear plastic rubbish from coasts.

[7] Plastic is convenient, but its long-term harm to oceans cannot be ignored. Small consistent changes in people’s shopping and daily habits can greatly reduce the flow of plastic waste into marine habitats and protect thousands of sea creatures.
