Community Gardens Bring Green Hope to Crowded Cities

Text 1 — Analytical English B2 Level

📚 🎯 🌱

[1] Hong Kong and other dense Asian metropolises are packed with skyscrapers, leaving limited open green land for city residents. Many urban people have never watched crops grow or touched fresh soil in their daily lives. To fix this shortage of natural space, local NGOs and district councils have built hundreds of rooftop and vacant-lot community gardens across residential areas in recent years. These shared planting plots offer city dwellers easy access to nature and teach sustainable food growing knowledge.

[2] Mr Chan, a retired biology teacher, runs a rooftop community farm in Mong Kok. Before renovation, the rooftop was a messy storage space full of broken furniture and waste. Now over sixty local families rent small plots to grow seasonal leaf vegetables. All fertilisers used are organic, recycled from restaurant food waste, with no harmful chemical pesticides added to the soil.

[3] Community gardens bring two clear mental benefits to busy city residents. First, tending plants for one hour each weekend eases heavy exam or work stress and calms anxious minds. Unlike solo phone scrolling that isolates people, group planting lets neighbours chat and build genuine friendly connections while working side by side.

[4] Second, these shared farms raise public food awareness. Most teenagers only see wrapped vegetables in supermarkets and have no idea how seeds turn into edible crops. Community growing lets them witness the full plant growth cycle and learn to cut household food waste by reusing scraps as natural fertiliser.

[5] However, urban community gardens face two major practical barriers. First, suitable empty rooftop spaces are extremely rare in old residential buildings. Landlords worry soil weight and water leakage will crack old stone walls, so they refuse to rent rooftops for planting. Second, many families drop their rented plots halfway as heavy mock exams or overtime work leave no spare time to water crops.

[6] District Councillors and NGOs have created solutions to lower these obstacles. Professional building safety inspections ease landlords’ worries about wall damage. Volunteer watering shift groups take care of plants when plot owners are occupied with study or work. Most community gardens only run three-hour planting sessions at weekends to avoid taking up residents’ whole day.

[7] Community farming is not just a temporary urban trend. If more idle city land is opened for shared planting, citizens will rely less on imported produce and form greener daily lifestyles that protect both personal mental health and the city’s urban ecosystem.
