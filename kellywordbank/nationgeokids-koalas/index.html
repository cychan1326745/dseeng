Koalas: Australia's Eucalyptus Marsupials

Text 1 — Nat Geo Kids Adapted B2 Reading

🐨 🌿 🇦🇺

[1] Many people mistakenly call koalas "koala bears", but these fluffy grey animals are not bears at all. They belong to a special group of mammals called marsupials, which raise their newborn babies inside a soft pouch on the mother's belly. Wild koalas only live in eucalyptus woodlands across eastern Australia, where every part of their daily life depends fully on eucalyptus trees. Their thick woolly grey fur keeps them warm during cold mountain nights and shields their skin from harsh hot sunlight in summer.

[2] Eucalyptus leaves are the only food koalas will eat, and this strange diet shapes nearly all their habits. The leaves contain natural poison and extremely low calories, so koalas must save energy wherever possible. An adult koala eats roughly one kilogram of eucalyptus foliage each day, then sleeps 18 to 22 hours straight to conserve limited energy supplies. The word "koala" comes from an old Aboriginal language phrase meaning "no drink", because koalas rarely climb down to waterholes; they absorb almost all needed liquid from moist eucalyptus leaves.

[3] Koala family life follows a slow, gentle cycle. A baby koala, named a joey, is born tiny, blind and hairless after just one month of development inside its mother. Using strong smell and touch, the newborn crawls from the birth opening straight into its mother's pouch, where it attaches to a milk teat for six months. Once the joey grows fur and opens its eyes, it rides on its mum's back for another half-year, learning which eucalyptus leaves are safe to eat and how to grip tree branches with clawed hands.

[4] Sharp physical traits help koalas live high in treetops. Each hand has two separate thumbs, plus rough padded palms and curved claws to lock onto slippery bark. On their hind feet, two toes grow fused together into a single grooming claw, which they use to comb tangled fur and remove insects. Koalas are highly territorial; adult males mark their "home trees" with scent gland secretions and will chase unfamiliar koalas away from their feeding and resting zones.

[5] Three major human threats push wild koala numbers steadily down. First, farmers and city developers clear eucalyptus forests to build housing and crop fields, destroying the only habitat koalas can survive in. Second, hotter, longer droughts and massive bushfires caused by climate change burn millions of eucalyptus trees and leave koalas with no food or shelter. Third, a widespread bacterial disease called chlamydia spreads quickly through wild populations, making females unable to produce healthy joeys.

[6] Australian wildlife groups run targeted koala rescue and protection work. Wildlife hospitals treat sick and injured koalas, rehabilitate orphaned joeys, and release recovered animals into protected eucalyptus reserves. National park laws ban tree clearing inside core koala living zones, and local councils plant thousands of native eucalyptus saplings to rebuild lost forest corridors between separated wild groups. Eco-tour guides teach visitors to never touch wild koalas and stay quiet near treetop resting spots.

[7] Koalas are beloved global wildlife icons, but their survival relies entirely on intact eucalyptus forests. Teenagers can join tree-planting volunteer days, share koala conservation facts with family, and avoid supporting brands that source timber from Australian native woodland clearing. Protecting koalas means preserving whole Australian forest ecosystems for birds, insects and other small marsupials that share eucalyptus habitats.
