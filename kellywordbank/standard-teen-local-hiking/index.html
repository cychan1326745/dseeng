Text 1 — HK Standard B2 Level

🥾 ⛰️ 🌿

[1] Many Hong Kong teenagers believe outdoor activities such as hiking are time-consuming and tiring, especially when they are buried under endless mock exams and tutorial classes. Most young people fill their spare time with scrolling social media or playing mobile games indoors. Few realise that nearly 40 per cent of Hong Kong’s land area is country park, with dozens of easy coastal and mountain trails suitable for secondary students to explore on weekends. Local youth organisations are now encouraging teens to go hiking instead of staying at home all day.

[2] Kevin, a Form 5 student who takes regular hiking trips with his classmates, shares his experience of Dragon’s Back, Asia’s most famous urban hiking trail. Before he picked up this hobby, he often felt anxious and short-tempered from heavy academic pressure. Every Saturday morning, his group takes a short bus ride to the trailhead and walks for around two hours. The route has gentle slopes and amazing sea views of Shek O and the South China Sea, which quickly calms his exam-related stress.

[3] Hiking brings two major physical benefits to teenagers. First, long hours of sitting in classrooms lead to stiff necks and back pain; regular walking stretches muscles and improves blood circulation. Second, spending hours outdoors lets students absorb natural daylight, which helps fix irregular sleep schedules caused by late-night phone scrolling. Researchers from a local university found that teens who hike at least once a week fall asleep faster at night and feel more energetic during daytime lessons.

[4] Apart from physical improvements, hiking also shapes young people’s social skills. Unlike solo screen time that isolates individuals, group trails require constant communication. Hikers need to remind each other about safety, share drinking water and help slower classmates climb steep sections. These small cooperative moments build genuine friendship rather than shallow online chats that lack real face-to-face interaction.

[5] However, many families still hold misunderstandings about teen hiking. The most common worry is safety risk: parents fear students will get lost or suffer injuries on remote hills. Another concern is that half-day hiking takes up valuable revision time before major examinations. Some parents even ban their kids from joining hiking teams completely to avoid these supposed drawbacks.

[6] To ease parents’ worries, youth hiking groups have set strict safety rules. All trips must be led by two trained adult volunteers who carry first-aid kits. Participants are asked to share their real-time location via mobile apps throughout the journey. Organisers also design short, beginner-level routes close to MTR stations, so teens do not need to travel far into isolated countryside areas. Each hiking activity only lasts three hours at most, leaving plenty of afternoon time for homework and revision.

[7] Hiking is more than just exercise. It lets city teenagers escape crowded skyscrapers and connect with Hong Kong’s natural landscape. Balancing study with short outdoor walks helps young people maintain both physical and mental health in such a high-pressure exam city.
