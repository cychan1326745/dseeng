The Standard (HK Standard) Adapted B2 Reading Passage (Digital Citizenship & Teen Screen Addiction)

Text 1 — HK Standard B2 Level

📱 🧠 🔒

[1] Local university researchers recently published a two-year study on Hong Kong secondary students’ digital habits. They surveyed more than 1,800 youngsters and found that over half spent more than three hours every day scrolling social media or playing online games. Many teachers and parents blame unlimited screen time for poor exam results, bad sleep and weak real-life communication skills. Yet the research team claims simple time limits are not enough to solve this growing youth problem. Instead, teenagers need digital competence training at school.

[2] Digital competence means critical, thoughtful use of digital tools, not just learning how to operate apps. Students with strong digital skills can judge false online news, protect their private data and stay away from cyberbullying. Those who lack such ability easily fall into gaming addiction or become targets of online verbal abuse. Dr Wong, the lead researcher of the project, points out that teens who understand online risks do not give up screens entirely — they use technology in balanced, purposeful ways.

[3] The study also discovered two clear links between low digital skills and harmful online behaviour. First, students with weak digital judgement often share personal photos and contact details on public platforms, which invites privacy leaks and online harassment. Second, they cannot separate entertainment from study; pop-up game notifications break their focus during homework, lowering their learning efficiency day by day.

[4] Many local schools already set strict phone bans in classrooms, but this rule has obvious limits. Students simply hide their devices in schoolbags and scroll secretly during breaks or lunch. Banning phones cannot teach young people self-control. By contrast, digital citizenship lessons let students discuss online risks openly in class, so they learn self-regulation without constant teacher supervision.

[5] Some parents worry digital training will encourage kids to use phones more often. Dr Wong disagrees with this fear. She explains that competence lessons do not promote screen time. They teach teenagers to tell useful digital resources from meaningless short videos. After joining related workshops, most participants cut passive scrolling time by nearly half, while keeping tech for school research and contacting distant relatives.

[6] Schools can carry out simple digital education activities without heavy extra costs. Teachers can hold monthly group discussions about cyberbullying and fake news. They can also invite IT social workers to deliver free after-school workshops. The research team’s final suggestion is to add digital citizenship into regular general studies lessons, so every student receives consistent guidance throughout their secondary school years.

[7] In the long run, limiting screen hours only treats the surface symptom of youth tech addiction. Building solid digital competence addresses the root cause: teenagers do not know how to tell right from wrong in the online world. If schools and families work together on this training, young people can enjoy technology without suffering its negative side effects.
