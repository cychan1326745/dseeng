[2] Coral reefs carry the nickname “rainforests of the sea” because of extraordinary biodiversity. Less than one percent of the ocean floor holds reefs, yet they supply shelter and food for around 25% of all marine species. Sea turtles, seahorses, giant clams and hundreds of reef fish hide between coral branches to avoid larger ocean predators.

[3] A symbiotic partnership keeps corals healthy and bright. Tiny microscopic algae called zooxanthellae live inside polyp tissue. The algae create food via photosynthesis and share nutrients with the coral host. In exchange, corals give algae a safe protected home. These algae produce all vivid red, blue and orange coral colours. When water warms too much, corals expel algae and turn pale white – a damaging process called coral bleaching.

[4] Australia’s Great Barrier Reef is Earth’s largest coral system. It stretches 2,300 kilometres and can be seen from outer space. The oldest reef rock layers date back over 240 million years, long before dinosaurs existed. More than 800 types of hard coral grow within this landmark.

[5] Three major human-caused dangers destroy modern reefs: rising ocean temperatures from climate change, farm chemical and plastic coastal pollution, plus careless boat anchors and destructive fishing techniques that smash fragile coral into rubble. Mass bleaching events now happen every few years across tropical oceans.

[6] Reef loss brings severe trouble for humans. Roughly 500 million people depend on reef fish as their primary food supply. Reef rock acts as natural sea breakers that absorb storm wave power and prevent coastal floods and soil erosion. Without reefs, seaside towns face stronger typhoons and seafood shortages.

[7] Communities and scientists carry out reef restoration. Researchers grow young coral fragments in underwater nurseries then plant broken reef beds. Governments create marine protected zones that ban harmful fishing and boat activities. Ordinary citizens can help by cutting single-use plastic and choosing sustainably caught seafood.
