Teen Street Musicians: Urban Street Art

Text 1 — Studio Classroom B2 Level

📚 🎵 🌍

[1] When Ivy Chen walked out of her school building after class one afternoon, she saw Jonah Park sitting on stone steps playing the violin. He was fully absorbed in his music and barely noticed passers-by. Ivy sat down quietly to listen and praised his beautiful melody after he finished the piece. This meeting started their long friendship built around street performance art.

[2] Street music, also called busking, is a special form of public art. Teen musicians set up simple equipment on city pavements: string instruments, small speakers and open instrument cases for voluntary tips. Unlike formal concert halls with expensive tickets, street performances are free for every citizen, including low-income children and elderly retirees who cannot afford live music shows.

[3] Many parents worry busking distracts teenagers from school revision, yet Jonah proves street music brings multiple positive values. First, regular public performances build strong confidence. Playing before crowds teaches young artists to stay calm under pressure and accept different audiences’ opinions. Second, busking improves music skills rapidly; frequent live practice fixes weak points that slow classroom progress.

[4] Third, street musicians connect with diverse city people. Tourists, office workers and local seniors stop to listen and chat with teens after performances. These short conversations help young performers understand different life stories and broaden their social horizons. Some teen buskers also raise small charity donations from listeners for animal shelters or youth mental groups.

[5] Still, street artists face clear limits set by city rules. Local councils require buskers to apply for official permits before playing music in public areas. No loud sound is allowed during late night hours to avoid disturbing nearby residents. Some busy shopping zones ban street performances completely to keep smooth pedestrian flow.

[6] Jonah follows all official rules strictly. He only performs between 4 p.m. and 8 p.m., uses low-volume speakers and never blocks walkways. He also donates half of his daily tip money to a local youth mental health charity. Ivy often joins him to hand out charity leaflets and talk to listeners about supporting teen welfare projects.

[7] Street busking is more than a hobby for young artists. It brings confidence, musical growth and community warmth to busy modern cities, as long as performers respect public rules and care for local people’s daily lives.
