Gentle Sea Cows – Manatees

Text 1 — Nat Geo Kids Adapted B2 Reading

🐋 🌊 🪫

[1] Manatees are large, slow-moving marine mammals often nicknamed "sea cows". They live in warm shallow freshwater rivers, coastal lagoons and tropical ocean bays across Florida, the Caribbean and West Africa. Unlike fish, manatees must swim to the water surface every three to five minutes to breathe air through their round blowholes on top of their heads. Their grey wrinkled skin is covered in thin short hair, and algae often grow on their backs, turning their bodies a greenish-brown shade.

[2] These peaceful animals are strict herbivores, meaning they only eat underwater plants. A single adult manatee can graze on up to 50 kilograms of seagrass and water lettuce every single day. Their flat, wide molar teeth constantly grow and wear down from grinding tough plant stems, so they never run out of teeth to feed. Since manatees spend most of their time feeding near the water's surface, they are easy to spot floating in calm, warm waters with lots of aquatic vegetation.

[3] Manatees have no natural predators in their habitats, yet human activity has pushed them to the edge of extinction. Fast motorboat propellers cut deep scars into their soft bodies, causing serious injuries or slow painful death. Many waterways are blocked by fishing nets, which trap manatees underwater and stop them from reaching the surface to breathe. Cold winter weather is another hidden danger: if water temperatures drop below 20°C, manatees lose energy and suffer life-threatening cold stress.

[4] Warm natural springs act as vital winter shelters for Florida manatees. These underground spring streams stay at a steady 22°C all year long. Hundreds of manatees gather in spring sanctuaries between December and March to escape cold coastal seawater. Local wildlife parks build viewing boardwalks beside spring pools, letting visitors watch manatees without stepping into their resting zones.

[5] Governments worldwide have introduced strict protection laws to save manatee populations. All speed limits for motorboats are lowered in manatee living zones to reduce propeller strikes. Fishing gear that can trap marine mammals is banned in lagoons and rivers. Protected wildlife sanctuaries mark off spring feeding and resting areas where boats and swimmers are completely forbidden to enter.

[6] Local rescue teams run manatee rehabilitation centres for injured or cold-stressed individuals. Vets treat propeller wounds and nurse weak manatees back to health with fresh seagrass and warm controlled pools. Once they fully recover, healthy manatees are released back into safe protected waterways with plenty of plant food and low boat traffic.

[7] Ordinary teens can help protect wild manatees without visiting coastal areas. Share boat speed rules with family members who own watercraft, avoid buying seafood caught with harmful drift nets, and donate to wildlife rescue charities. Manatees play a key role in balancing wetland ecosystems: heavy grazing keeps seagrass beds thin enough for sunlight to reach small water creatures, supporting the whole coastal food chain.
