What makes a modern-day curriculum?

Text 1 — DSE-style Reading Practice

📚 🎓 📊

[1] When we hear the word “curriculum”, most of us picture textbooks, strict exam-focused lessons and long lists of subjects set by the government. But today’s educators are rethinking what should be included within a school curriculum, arguing that traditional schooling fails to prepare teenagers for real-life challenges. I spoke with Dr Helen Shaw, an educational researcher who has spent 15 years studying curriculum reform across Europe, to find out her views.

[2] Interviewer: Why do you think the old-style curriculum has become outdated?
Dr Shaw: Back 30 years ago, schools mainly focused on academic knowledge like history, maths and literature. The curriculum was designed to produce office-workers and factory employees. Now young people face a rapidly-changing job market with AI and digital technology. Hard-earned textbook knowledge might become out-of-date within just a few years. That is why narrow book-learning alone is no longer enough.

[3] Interviewer: What new skills should schools add to the official curriculum?
Dr Shaw: First, practical life-skills are badly overlooked. Things like managing personal budgets, understanding mental-health problems and handling online information are essential. Many young people can score high exam marks but struggle to tell true news apart from fake content online. Another key addition is emotional intelligence, or EQ. Students need to learn how to cooperate with teammates and deal with pressure. These soft-skills were once seen as parents’ responsibility, yet schools now have to step in.

[4] Interviewer: Some parents complain that adding these topics will reduce time for exam-related subjects. What is your response to this?
Dr Shaw: Their worry is understandable, but it rests on a false assumption. People think EQ lessons take time away from studying, but actually they complement each other. Less anxious students can concentrate better during class and get higher grades. Besides, a well-rounded curriculum does not mean cutting core subjects completely; it means rearranging lesson time wisely.

[5] Interviewer: Do you believe this new-type curriculum will become widely accepted soon?
Dr Shaw: Progress is slow. Conservative-minded policymakers still value exam results above everything else. However, forward-thinking schools in Finland and Germany have already updated their curriculum and achieved promising outcomes. I hope more governments can learn from these successful examples in the next decade.

[6] Interviewer: Overall, what do you hope a revised curriculum can bring to teenagers?
Dr Shaw: My ultimate goal is simple. A good curriculum should not only train smart exam-takers, but also independent, thoughtful young adults who can adapt themselves to our fast-changing world.
