Text 1 — SCMP Young Post B2 Level

[1] Every weekday, thousands of Hong Kong teenagers and working adults squeeze onto packed MTR trains, buses and minibuses to travel to school or offices. Many spend up to two hours each day travelling between their homes in the New Territories and urban city centres. Most people treat long commutes as an unavoidable routine, yet few realise how badly daily transit damages their mental and physical health. Psychologists call this “commuter stress”, a widespread issue that is often overlooked by the public.

[2] The biggest drawback of long travel hours is lost personal time. When commuters waste one or two hours every single day on transport, they have far less space for rest, hobbies, family chats and self-care. Over months and years, this shortage of free time creates constant tiredness, short tempers and a lower standard of daily life. Traffic jams and overcrowded carriages make the situation even worse. During rush hours, passengers are forced into tight spaces with strangers, which causes sensory overload and raises anxiety, especially for those who dislike being surrounded by crowds.

[3] Another source of commuter stress is unpredictable delays. Train signal faults, road accidents or sudden heavy rain can hold travellers back without warning. To avoid being late for school or work, most people have to leave their houses much earlier than needed. This cuts further into their sleep hours and leaves them exhausted before their day even starts. Research from local youth mental groups shows that students with long daily journeys are more likely to suffer from sleep loss and poor focus during lessons.

[4] Long commutes also harm physical well-being. Sitting still for hours every day leads to stiff shoulders, back pain and poor blood circulation. Many commuters rely on sugary snacks and fast food to pass travel time, which creates unhealthy eating habits. Experts add that repeated daily stress from crowded transport keeps stress hormones at high levels in the human body, raising long-term risks of headaches and low mood disorders such as mild depression.

[5] However, simple daily habits can ease commuter pressure without changing where you live. Many regular travellers practise mindful breathing while waiting for trains or stuck in traffic. The 4-7-8 breathing method – inhale for four seconds, hold for seven and exhale for eight – calms the nervous system and reduces frustration quickly. Others bring light reading materials or soft music to turn empty travel time into relaxing personal moments.

[6] City planners are also taking action to improve commuting experiences. New MTR extension lines connect remote residential districts to central areas, cutting average journey times by nearly 40 minutes. Some bus companies have introduced quieter carriages reserved for passengers who want to study or rest without noise. These small adjustments aim to reduce the mental burden of daily travel for all citizens.
