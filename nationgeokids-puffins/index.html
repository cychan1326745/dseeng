Puffins – Sea Parrots of Northern Ocean Cliffs

Text 3 — Nat Geo Kids Adapted B2 Reading

🐧 🌊 🪨

[1] Puffins are small black-and-white seabirds famous for their huge colourful orange, red and yellow beaks, earning them the cute nickname "sea parrots". They spend eight months of every year floating and hunting out on the cold open North Atlantic and Arctic oceans, only returning to rocky coastal cliff tops during short summer breeding season. In winter, their bright patterned beaks fade to dull grey and shrink slightly; the vivid multi-coloured bill only grows bright again each spring to attract mating partners.

[2] Puffins have perfectly adapted bodies for both flying and diving. Short strong wings beat fast to lift their small bodies above ocean waves, while underwater those same wings act as flippers to swim deep down after fish. They can dive to depths of 60 metres and hold their breath underwater for up to one minute. Their special ridged tongue and sharp inner beak spikes lock small fish tightly inside their mouths, so they can carry dozens of sand eels back to cliff nests in one single trip without dropping prey.

[3] Every summer, thousands of puffins crowd onto bare rocky island cliffs to dig underground nesting burrows. They use sharp claws and strong beaks to dig one-metre-long tunnels into soft cliff soil. Each pair of puffins lays just one single white egg per year inside the dark burrow. Both parents share incubation shifts, sitting on the egg for around forty days until the fluffy grey chick hatches. For six weeks after hatching, mum and dad puffins take turns diving to catch fish to feed their growing baby.

[4] Three main human-caused threats have cut global puffin numbers sharply over the past 30 years. Warming ocean temperatures from climate change push small fish like sand eels far away from cliff breeding grounds, leaving puffins with little food to feed their chicks. Plastic waste floating on the sea surface poisons puffins that mistake colourful plastic fragments for edible fish. Overfishing by large commercial boats also removes massive amounts of the tiny sea creatures puffins rely on as their main diet.

[5] Northern European and Canadian wildlife groups run dedicated puffin conservation programmes. Protected cliff nesting islands ban all tourist landing during breeding months to avoid frightening parent birds away from their eggs. Strict fishing quotas limit how many small sand eels commercial boats can catch each year, keeping enough fish stock for wild seabirds. Beach clean-up volunteer events remove tons of floating ocean plastic that endangers diving puffins.

[6] Citizen science projects invite ordinary teenagers and visitors to help monitor puffin colonies. People join organised cliff observation walks to count nesting pairs and record chick survival rates each summer. All collected bird population data is shared with marine biologists to track long-term puffin population rises and falls across the North Atlantic ocean.

[7] Puffins are important indicators of ocean health. If local puffin numbers drop quickly, it signals that ocean fish stocks are shrinking or seawater temperatures are rising dangerously fast. Protecting puffin nesting cliffs, limiting overfishing and clearing marine plastic waste helps balance cold northern ocean food webs and supports hundreds of other seabird and marine species that share the same ocean habitat.
