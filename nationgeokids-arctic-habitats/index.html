Reading Solution

Save Our Polar Arctic Habitats

Text 2 — Nat Geo Kids Adapted B2 Reading

🐻‍❄️ ❄️ 🧊

[1] The Arctic region covers the top of our planet, made up of frozen ocean sea ice and cold surrounding land. This icy wilderness is home to countless special animals that rely entirely on stable ice to live, hunt and raise their young. Polar bears, narwhals, arctic foxes and snow hares all have unique physical adaptations to survive freezing temperatures below minus thirty degrees Celsius. However, global climate change steadily melts sea ice each year, destroying the foundation of the Arctic food chain.

[2] Polar bears are classified as marine mammals, because they spend most of their lives travelling across floating sea ice. Their thick layer of fat and water-repellent double fur lock in body heat, while wide black ears disperse extra warmth into cold air. Polar bears travel dozens of kilometres swimming to reach new ice platforms where they hunt seals, their main source of food. When ice melts early in spring, bears get trapped on land with no way to find prey and slowly starve.

[3] Narwhals are nicknamed the unicorns of the sea due to the long spiral ivory tusk grown by male individuals. This tusk can stretch over three metres long and scientists believe males use it to compete for female narwhals or sense changes in ocean water. These pale porpoises travel in groups of twenty or more, diving deep beneath ice sheets to catch fish and squid. Melting ice reduces their hidden feeding spaces and exposes them to more hunting threats.

[4] Arctic foxes and snow hares change their fur colour with the seasons for natural camouflage. In winter their coats turn pure white to blend into snow; when summer arrives, fur shifts to brown and grey to match bare tundra ground. Foxes follow polar bears to feed on leftover seal meat scraps, forming a quiet dependent relationship between species. Without stable ice and seasonal snow cover, their camouflage trick stops working and they become easy targets for larger predators.

[5] Two major human activities speed up Arctic ice loss. Burning fossil fuels for electricity, transport and factory work releases massive carbon dioxide that traps heat in Earth's atmosphere. Secondly, industrial oil and gas drilling in the Arctic damages ice surfaces and creates toxic spills that poison all marine life. Wildlife researchers report steady population drops for nearly all Arctic species over the past fifty years.

[6] Global governments have created joint protection plans to slow Arctic warming. Many nations signed agreements to limit fossil fuel production near polar zones and ban unregulated Arctic oil drilling. Scientists also carry out long-term ice observation missions to track melting rates and share data worldwide.

[7] Young people can join small daily actions to cut carbon emissions and protect the Arctic. Reduce electricity waste at home, choose walking or cycling over private car rides, and support brands that use 100% renewable wind or solar power. Every small cut in greenhouse gas output slows ice melt and buys more time for Arctic wild animals to adapt.
