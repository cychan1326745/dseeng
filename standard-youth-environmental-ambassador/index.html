Text 1 — HK Standard B2 Level

🌿 🦅 ♻️

[1] Many Hong Kong people do not realise the city holds rich wildlife resources, including nearly 600 bird species and over 6,000 marine creatures. Yet urban construction, river pollution and discarded plastic waste threaten local plants and animals every year. To raise public awareness of biodiversity protection, local NGO Civic Exchange launched the Young Environment Ambassador (YEA) programme back in 2020, inviting secondary students to carry out eco projects in their spare time.

[2] Valent Shen, an 18-year-old ecology undergraduate, joined the programme when he was still a Secondary Four student. He has a deep passion for Hong Kong’s wild creatures and spends most of his weekends taking photos of birds, reptiles and amphibians across country parks. He once held a wildlife photography exhibition at the Maritime Museum, where over 800 visitors viewed his shots. His main goal was to show both locals and tourists the wide variety of native wildlife most people overlook in busy city life.

[3] During the YEA training sessions, teenagers learn practical eco knowledge that rarely appears in standard school textbooks. Unlike regular geography lessons that only talk about global climate change in abstract terms, the programme focuses on local environmental problems, such as polluted Tuen Mun rivers and plastic waste piled up on coastal shores. Each ambassador must finish one community project before graduation from the scheme.

[4] Valent chose to run a free wildlife sharing workshop for primary school kids. He brought his photo collection to primary campuses and taught children simple ways to protect small creatures: avoiding throwing rubbish into streams and not touching wild insects or reptiles out of curiosity. Some young pupils used to catch frogs for fun before the workshop, but they promised to leave wildlife undisturbed after listening to Valent’s sharing.

[5] Though the YEA scheme gains positive feedback from schools, it still faces two major limits. First, the NGO only hires a small team of trainers, so the number of yearly participants is capped at 120 students. Second, most families prioritise exam revision over voluntary eco work; many teens give up halfway when mock exam seasons arrive, as their parents worry about lost study hours.

[6] Lawrence Iu, executive director of Civic Exchange, says schools should integrate more local environmental content into daily lessons. At present, climate education in Hong Kong schools stays too general, with few field trips to wetlands or coastal areas. If students can experience native wildlife in person, they will form a long-lasting sense of responsibility to protect their home city’s natural surroundings.

[7] Valent believes youth power can create real change. He encourages all secondary students to take small daily eco actions, such as cutting single-use plastic and joining local clean-up activities. Even tiny consistent efforts from thousands of teenagers can slow down biodiversity loss in Hong Kong.
