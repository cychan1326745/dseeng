Desert Camels: Built to Survive Extreme Dry Heat

Text 3 — Nat Geo Kids Adapted B2 Reading

🐪 🏜️ ☀️

[1] Two main types of camels live in Earth's dry desert biomes: single-humped dromedaries found across the Middle East and North Africa, and twin-humped Bactrian camels native to cold Central Asian deserts. These large hoofed mammals are perfectly engineered to survive extreme temperature swings, scarce fresh water and endless sandstorms that would kill most other animals. Humans have worked alongside camels for over 3,000 years, using them as reliable transport and cargo carriers across barren desert trade routes.

[2] The camel's most famous feature, its hump, stores thick fatty tissue — not water, as many people mistakenly believe. This stored fat acts as long-term energy storage when food and plants are hard to find on empty sand plains. When the camel uses up its fat reserves during long journeys, its hump shrinks and flops sideways. A single healthy camel can survive up to three weeks without eating solid food by slowly burning its hump fat for energy.

[3] Camels hold special hidden adaptations to store drinking water efficiently. After weeks of dehydration, one camel can drink over 130 litres of water in just ten minutes. The water spreads into special flexible blood cells and stomach pouches instead of being lost quickly through sweat. Unlike humans and other mammals, camels barely sweat even in 45°C desert heat, keeping precious liquid locked inside their bodies for slow gradual use.

[4] Multiple body parts protect camels from blinding sandstorms. Double rows of thick eyelashes block fine sand grains from reaching their eyes. Narrow slit nostrils can close completely to stop dust rushing into airways. Thick woolly fur insulates their skin: it reflects burning sunlight during scorching daytime heat and traps warm air around their bodies during freezing desert nights. Wide padded flat hooves spread their heavy weight across loose sand to stop them sinking into deep dunes.

[5] Modern human development threatens wild camel populations across Asia and Africa. Large desert mining projects dig up natural desert plant feeding grounds and pollute rare underground water sources. Fast motorised trucks replace camels for cargo transport, so local herders stop feeding and protecting wild camel herds. Tourist off-road vehicles drive freely across sand dunes, destroying sparse desert grass that camels rely on for food. Wild Bactrian camels are now listed as critically endangered by global wildlife groups.

[6] International wildlife organisations run targeted camel protection schemes. Protected desert wildlife zones ban mining and unrestricted off-road driving. Local herder families receive financial support to keep raising and guarding camel herds, preserving ancient human-camel partnerships. Scientists dig artificial underground water wells inside reserves to replace polluted natural desert water holes for wild camels to drink.

[7] Camels are irreplaceable desert keystone animals. They spread plant seeds across vast sand dunes through their waste, helping sparse desert vegetation regrow in empty areas. Without camels moving seeds, desert plant life would shrink rapidly, removing the only food source for dozens of smaller desert creatures. Saving camels means protecting the whole fragile dry desert food chain for future generations.
