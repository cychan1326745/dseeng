SCMP Young Post Original Adapted B2 Reading Passage (Student Mental Health Campaign)

Text 1 — SCMP Young Post B2 Level

🧠 💪 ❤️

[1] Charlotte Yuen was just an ordinary Secondary Three student when she was trapped in a cycle of severe exam anxiety and perfectionism. Every time exams drew near, she lost sleep, felt constant self-doubt and feared that any low score would make her worthless. Like thousands of Hong Kong teenagers, she hid her negative feelings from her parents and teachers, mistakenly believing that talking about stress meant she was weak and incapable.

[2] The turning point arrived when Charlotte visited her school social worker for regular counselling. During their talks, she learned that mental struggles were nothing to be ashamed of, and sharing emotions could ease loneliness. After her recovery, she launched a youth campaign named Be Positive HK to break the stigma around teenage mental health. Now aged 18, Charlotte is a finalist of the Spirit of Hong Kong Youth Awards for this voluntary project.

[3] Be Positive HK reaches young people mainly via social media platforms, offline workshops and peer storytelling sessions. At each workshop, teens are invited to share their personal struggles with academic pressure, body image worries or social media comparison. Charlotte does not invite professional psychologists to give long lectures filled with cold statistics. Instead, the campaign focuses on real student stories, which participants find much easier to relate to.

[4] Many existing school mental health programmes rely heavily on data and medical knowledge, yet they fail to connect with young audiences. A local clinical psychologist explains that facts alone rarely comfort anxious teens. Personal narratives help young readers realise they are not the only ones going through tough times. However, Charlotte stresses that her campaign never promises a perfect, happy ending for everyone’s mental journey. Some recovery processes take years, and it is completely normal to stay in a difficult middle stage without instant relief.

[5] Schools have gradually welcomed Be Positive HK’s workshops into their campuses. Still, a large number of secondary students refuse to join these activities, holding two common misunderstandings. First, they think only students with serious mental illnesses need support groups. Second, they worry their classmates will judge them negatively if they open up about their inner worries during group sharing.

[6] To remove these barriers, Charlotte’s team designed relaxed, low-pressure activities such as anonymous note-writing. Students can write down their stress on slips of paper without signing their names and place them in a shared box. Volunteers then read out the anonymous stories without revealing identities. This method lets teens release their feelings without fearing embarrassment or gossip at school.

[7] Charlotte’s long-term goal is to build a school culture where mental health conversations become ordinary. She hopes that future generations of students will no longer view anxiety as a shameful secret, but treat it as a common life challenge that everyone deserves support to overcome.
